Over the past two weeks, we've deconstructed the math of RWKV (Part 1) and built a complete implementation in pure PyTorch (Part 2).

Now, the benchmark that makes the case for why RWKV actually matters.

I pitted our custom RWKV model against standard Transformer and LSTM baselines on synthetic sequence modeling tasks. All models were matched for parameter count (~50K params). Training was run for 50 epochs on next-token prediction.

📊 **The Training Convergence:**
- Transformer: Final Test Loss 0.267, Accuracy 88.9%
- RWKV: Final Test Loss 0.289, Accuracy 87.3%
- LSTM: Final Test Loss 0.378, Accuracy 82.1%

RWKV closes the gap with the Transformer significantly while outperforming the LSTM. The slight accuracy trade-off is expected—linear attention is an approximation of full attention.

But here's where RWKV destroys the competition:

⚡ **Inference Latency (ms per token):**
At sequence length 32:
- RWKV: 0.42 ms | Transformer: 0.38 ms | LSTM: 0.51 ms

At sequence length 256:
- RWKV: 0.45 ms | Transformer: 1.43 ms | LSTM: 0.58 ms

RWKV's latency is CONSTANT regardless of sequence length. The Transformer's latency grows 3.8× because the KV cache grows linearly.

💾 **Memory Usage During Inference:**
At sequence length 256:
- RWKV: 12.8 MB
- Transformer: 67.5 MB
- LSTM: 13.7 MB

RWKV uses **5.3× less memory** than the Transformer at long sequence lengths. This is the O(1) inference promise in action.

🔑 **Why This Matters:**
For long-context generation (stories, documents, code), RWKV enables:
- 10K+ token contexts on consumer hardware
- Higher batch sizes during serving (more throughput)
- Edge deployment on memory-constrained devices
