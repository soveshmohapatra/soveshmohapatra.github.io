Deconstructing Tokenization

Part 3: Compression and Failure Modes

By Sovesh Mohapatra • May 2026
Overview

BPE trained on a 33-KB English corpus at four vocabulary sizes. The compression curve is universal; the failure mode is specific.

Compression Curve

Sharp initial improvement, sharply diminishing returns. The first 700 merges absorb most of the structural redundancy. Same shape as GPT-2's tokenizer trained on the Pile.

Every one of these is structurally driven: word boundaries, suffixes, and the most common word in English. BPE discovers linguistic structure through pure frequency counting.

The Failure Mode — Merge #200

The corpus deliberately contained a seven-line block of Python-like syntax repeated 80 times. After 200 merges, BPE produced a single token whose UTF-8 expansion is:

"model.parameters()\ntorch.nn.functional\nloss.backward()\noptimizer.step()\nfor epoch in range(num_epochs)"

116 characters of source code encoded as one token id. Because the same block appeared 80 times, BPE merged it from the bottom up — individual lines first, then pairs, then larger combinations.

This is exactly the pathology that affects production tokenizers when their training data contains repetitive code, log files, structured outputs, or commonly-repeated prompts. It explains why GPT-3 famously could not count letters in words (characters disappear into multi-char tokens) and why subtle whitespace changes alter LLM behavior.

Takeaway

BPE is greedy compression. It is not language understanding. Every LLM behaviour that depends on character counting, position-within-word, or sub-token reasoning is downstream of this design. The tokenizer is the silent foundation under everything.
