Deconstructing CLIP

Part 3: 100% Zero-Shot in 14 Seconds

By Sovesh Mohapatra • May 2026
Overview

3,200 32×32 RGB images, 16 classes (4 colors × 4 shapes). 75K-parameter CLIP, 30 epochs, 14 seconds on MPS. 100% zero-shot on the held-out test set.

Setup Recap

Task. 16-way classification of 32×32 RGB images. Random baseline: $6.25\%$. Data. $3{,}200$ synthetic image-caption pairs; $2{,}720$ train, $480$ held out. Model. ImageEncoder (5-conv CNN, 64-dim projection), TextEncoder (token+pos embed, mean pool, 64-dim projection), plus one learnable temperature. Total: $74{,}913$ parameters.

Epoch 1 already hits $27.1\%$ — four times the random baseline. The contrastive signal is dense: every batch is itself a 64-way classification problem.

What the Embedding Space Looks Like

A t-SNE projection of the held-out image embeddings (small dots) and the 16 class-caption embeddings (large stars) shows 16 tight clusters. Each contains the caption-star plus all $30$ images of that class. The 16-class structure emerged from the contrastive objective alone — the model was never told that "color" and "shape" are separable attributes.

Diagonal Dominance in the Similarity Matrix

The image-caption similarity matrix on the held-out batch shows the expected pattern: each row's maximum is on the diagonal with a clear gap to the runners-up. The off-diagonal structure is informative too — the same shape in a different color is the second-highest match, indicating the model encodes shape and color as somewhat independent dimensions.

Generalisation: What Scales

If we scaled this experiment up to OpenAI's CLIP — bigger encoders (ViT-B/32 or ResNet-50, ~100M params), bigger captions (variable-length natural language, vocab 50K), bigger batches ($32{,}768$ vs $64$), bigger corpus (400M pairs) — the algorithm doesn't change. The recipe scales by changing magnitudes.
