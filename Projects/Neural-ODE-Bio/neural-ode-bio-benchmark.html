Neural ODEs on Biological Dynamics

Part 3: Phase-Portrait Recovery and Why It Matters

By Sovesh Mohapatra • May 2026
Overview

Both Neural ODE and a parameter-matched discrete RNN fit FHN trajectories to MSE 0.02. Only the Neural ODE learned the physics.

Trajectory-Fit Results

Neural ODE$17{,}282$$0.02072$$0.02014$$40.5$ s
Discrete RNN$17{,}282$$0.01547$$0.01670$$8.9$ s

In-distribution the discrete RNN is marginally better and trains 4.5× faster. Extrapolation past the training horizon (4× longer trajectory): both stay close to their in-distribution MSE — neither model degrades dramatically. If we had kept $t$ in the Neural ODE input, extrapolation MSE would have been $\sim 5.0$ instead of $0.02$ — a factor of 250 difference from one design choice.

The Key Visual Finding — Phase-Portrait Recovery

Trajectory MSE is a noisy proxy for "did the model learn the dynamics." A model can fit individual time series without learning the underlying flow.

The phase-portrait visualisation answers the better question: query each model's predicted $f$ at a grid of $(v, w)$ points, compare against ground-truth $f_\text{FHN}$ arrow-by-arrow.

Neural ODE phase portrait

RegimeMean cosine similarity with $f_\text{FHN}$

The Neural ODE recovers the direction of the FHN flow with cosine similarity above $0.94$ in both regimes. Learned arrows visibly match ground-truth arrows.

Discrete RNN phase portrait

The RNN has no explicit $f$. Its residual update returns the time-integrated effect over one $\Delta t$. We can recover the implicit instantaneous flow by dividing by $\Delta t$ — but only because we know what $\Delta t$ was. Without that, the recovered "field" has correct direction but wrong magnitude.

What This Means for Biology

Biological data come at the instrument's sampling rate, not the dynamics' natural timescale. A discrete RNN trained on patch-clamp at 10 kHz produces a model that runs at 10 kHz steps. A Neural ODE trained on the same data lives in continuous time — the same model can predict at any rate, interpolate between samples, and be combined with other Neural ODEs that live in the same space.

The Headline Reframe

Time-series accuracy and physical fidelity are not the same metric.

A model that fits the data exactly but has learned a discrete-step approximation gives you predicted trajectories at the training rate. A model that learns the continuous-time vector field gives you a model of the system. They are different scientific objects, and only the second lets you reason about the underlying physics.
